# Unpack — one idea at a time, until it lands

Transfer understanding the operator could explain back to someone else — not the *feeling* of
being informed. You cannot transfer understanding by volume: the **wall of text** delivers
awareness and no comprehension. You transfer it one rung at a time, and **the operator does the
cognitive work** — you feed the right idea at the right altitude, then stop and confirm it
**landed** before adding the next. Never trade their comprehension for your speed.

## Step 1 — Build the ladder (privately, before you say anything)

Decompose the topic into a dependency-ordered sequence of single ideas, each resting on the ones
before. Order by understanding, not chronology or how you built it. Keep it to ~5–8 rungs; more
means you're mixing altitudes — collapse detail upward. A default skeleton (adapt freely):

1. **Why it exists** — the problem, as a plain mental model. (Almost always start here.)
2. **The core tension** — the one hard thing that makes this non-trivial.
3. **The mechanism** — how it works, high level.
4. **The constraints** — the guardrails that must hold.
5. **The evidence** — what was done / proof it works.
6. **The scrutiny** — how it was stress-tested, or where it's genuinely uncertain.
7. **The honest gaps** — what's *not* done. Glossing edges produces false confidence, worse than a
   known unknown ([[no-fabrication]]).

## Step 2 — Frame it, then find where they already are

Open with how this goes: layer by layer, one idea at a time, you'll stop and check, they set the
pace. Then locate their starting rung — ask ("how are you thinking about this now?") or deliver
rung 1 and invite "skip ahead if you know this." Never assume their start; never start above it.

## Step 3 — The per-turn loop (the heart of the skill)

For each rung, one message:

- **One idea. Only one.** The "and also…" you want to add is the next rung. Hold it.
- **Plain language.** Translate every term; name the technical word in parentheses *after* the
  plain version, never before.
- **Anchor to what they know.** Reach for an analogy from *their* world — their business, a process
  they run, something established earlier — not a generic one.
- **Then STOP on a check that makes them work** — a question ("in your own words, why X?"), a
  reaction gate ("does this land, or is anything fuzzy?"), or a predict ("what would you expect
  if…?"). The check is what *makes* them follow; skipping it because they seem to be keeping up
  defeats the skill.
- **Wait.** The next rung does not appear in this message.

## Step 4 — Calibrate to their response, every turn

- **"got it" / "next" / "skip"** → seal the prior point in one line so it isn't lost to speed, then
  advance one rung. Don't re-teach.
- **A wrong or partial check answer** → re-explain that rung a *different* way (new analogy, smaller
  piece), never the same explanation louder. Stay on the rung until it lands.
- **A question** → answer at their altitude, then resume where you were.
- **"too much" / overwhelmed** → you went too wide or deep. Drop to a smaller idea.

## Step 5 — Land it

After the last rung, give a compact recap of the full ladder in one tight list — a consolidation of
rungs already understood, not a fresh dump — so the layers fuse into one structure they can hold.
Then ask if any layer is still fuzzy before you call it done.

## Proactive offer (don't auto-run)

When you're about to dump a wall of text — a multi-step recap, a dense plan, a hairy bug, an
architecture — offer first: *"This is a lot — want me to walk you through it one layer at a time so
it lands?"* If they'd rather have it all at once, give it. Offer, don't impose.
